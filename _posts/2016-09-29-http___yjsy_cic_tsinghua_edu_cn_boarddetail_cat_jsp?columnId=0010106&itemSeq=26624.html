课程简介 Course Introduction ：
For various reasons, civil procedure can be studied from a European angle (and even from a global perspective). There are, however, other reasons for studying civil procedure in a European or even a global context. Within Europe, for example, it is still a matter of debate whether in the European Union procedural law should be harmonized to a greater extent as has been done up to now. In order to be able to take part in the debate on this issue, students should have some knowledge of (1) the present state of affairs as regards harmonization, (2) the existing national procedures in the Member States of the Union, (3) the origins of these procedures, and (4) the differences between these procedures and their similarities.
Apart from the relatively small number of cases covered by supra-national rules (e.g. the various European Regulations regulating procedural matters), the variety of national procedures seems bewildering at first sight. Nevertheless, some order to this chaos may be brought by distinguishing families of civil procedural law. At least two families of civil procedure may be distinguished within the European Union: the Common Law and Civil Law families. It is common to subdivide the second category into smaller families, such as the Germanic, Scandinavian and the Romanic family.
A considerable part of this course is devoted to comparing the different procedural families. Special attention is given to the historical development of the systems of civil procedure and to the question as to how these systems deal with the requirements of Article 6 of the European Convention on Human Rights and with fundamental principles of procedure in general. The final part of the course addresses the desirability of approximating procedural laws and, if the answer is in the affirmative, the way in which such approximation can best be achieved.
