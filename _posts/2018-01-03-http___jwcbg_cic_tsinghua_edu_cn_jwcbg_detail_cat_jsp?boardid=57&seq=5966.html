Beginning with the end in mind and designing toward that end

Th is session presented by the University of Connecticut’s Center for Excellence in Teaching and Learning will be a workshop focused on the use of backward design and Bloom’s taxonomy.   As a result of this workshop, participants will learn how to:

・          Develop measureable learning outcomes that focus on critical course content and skills;

・          Align course activities with learning outcomes; and

・          Design effective assessment strategies for evaluating student achievement of these learning outcomes

In advance of the workshop, faculty will be asked to complete pre-work related to their own experiences with well-designed learning experiences. They will also be asked to begin describing the ways they want their students to be different upon completion of their course. This will serve as the foundation for designing learning outcomes.

During the workshop, attendees will learn about and work through the 3 stages of backward design, including identifying desired results, determining acceptable evidence, and planning learning experiences and instruction. As part of identifying desired results, attendees will learn about and utilize Bloom’s taxonomy. The taxonomy provides a hierarchy of levels to assist faculty to focus on higher order thinking. By utilizing the taxonomy faculty can design learning experiences and performance tasks to achieve and measure higher order learning.

The workshop will utilize a workbook developed by the presenters. The workbook can be utilized by faculty who are unable to attend the workshop to develop their own learning objectives, activities, and assessment of learning.

 

Presenters:

Dr. Xinnian Chen is an Associate Professor in Residence in Physiology and Neurobiology, Associate Department Head for Undergraduate Programs, and Assistant Director of Faculty Development at CETL. Her research interests focus on the evaluation of teaching practice, investigation on how students learn in an active learning classroom setting, and assessment of student engagement and learning gains. During the summer, Dr. Chen directs the Northeast Summer Institute, a HHMI-funded program designed to provide crucial faculty development to refine classroom techniques. The institute provides faculty the opportunity to observe, evaluate, and collect innovative teaching approaches and instructional materials that are ready to be adopted and adapted to their own teaching environments.

Dr. Lauren Schlesselman is the Associate Director of the Center for Excellence in Teaching and Learning. In her role, she oversees faculty development initiatives and programming. She was named an UConn Teaching Scholar in 2014-15 for recognition of demonstrated commitment to continuously improving teaching through innovation and reflective practice and dedication to teaching effectiveness and enhanced student engagement. Previously, Dr. Schlesselman was the Associate Director for Academic Affairs in UConn’s School of Pharmacy, where her focus areas were curricular development, assessment of student learning, and leadership development.
